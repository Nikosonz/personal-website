## Technical SEO for Founders

- **Language:** English (en)
- **Slug:** `technical-seo-for-founders`
- **Excerpt:** The handful of technical fixes that actually move rankings — and the busywork you can safely ignore.
- **Meta description:** A founder's guide to technical SEO: the few fixes that move rankings (indexing, canonicals, speed, structure) and the busywork to skip.
- **Tags:** SEO, Technical SEO, Founders

**Body HTML** (paste into source mode):

```html
<p>Most founders I work with have been sold a version of SEO that is 90% busywork. They are tracking keyword densities and rewriting meta keywords — a tag Google stopped using in 2009 — while the actual problem is that half their pages aren&#39;t indexed. Technical SEO is not mystical. A small number of things matter a lot, and most of the rest is noise.</p>
<h2>First, confirm Google can even see you</h2>
<p>Before anything else, check what Google has indexed. Search <code>site:yourdomain.com</code>. If pages are missing, no amount of content or keywords will help — you have a visibility problem, not a ranking problem.</p>
<p>The usual culprits: a stray <code>noindex</code>, a <code>robots.txt</code> block, or pages that all canonicalize to the homepage (a single misconfigured tag can quietly tell Google every page is a duplicate). Fix indexing first. Everything else is downstream of it.</p>
<h2>The fixes that actually move rankings</h2>
<p><strong>1. One canonical per page.</strong> Each page should point its canonical at <em>itself</em>, not a shared URL. Get this wrong and Google collapses your whole site into one indexable page.</p>
<p><strong>2. Unique titles and descriptions.</strong> Every page needs its own. If your services page, contact page, and blog posts all share the homepage title, you have given Google nothing to differentiate them.</p>
<p><strong>3. Speed — but only the parts users feel.</strong> Core Web Vitals (largest contentful paint, interaction latency, layout shift) are real ranking inputs and, more importantly, real conversion inputs. The single biggest mistake I see: hero text that&#39;s invisible until JavaScript loads. Render your above-the-fold content server-side.</p>
<p><strong>4. A clean, logical URL structure.</strong> <code>example.com/seo/search-console-guide</code> tells a human and a crawler what the page is. <code>example.com/post?id=8393</code> tells them nothing.</p>
<p><strong>5. Structured data.</strong> Mark up who you are and what each page is (Person, Article, BreadcrumbList). It won&#39;t levitate you to position one, but it helps both Google and AI search engines understand and cite you.</p>
<p><strong>6. Internal links.</strong> Google discovers and weighs pages largely through the links between them. Link your pages to each other with descriptive anchor text — not &quot;click here.&quot;</p>
<h2>What you can safely ignore</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Meta keywords.</strong> Dead. Google ignores them entirely.</li>
<li><strong>Keyword stuffing.</strong> Actively harmful. Write for the reader.</li>
<li><strong>Domain extension.</strong> <code>.com</code> vs <code>.ir</code> vs <code>.org</code> gives you no ranking advantage. Pick what fits your brand.</li>
<li><strong>Volume for its own sake.</strong> Five genuinely useful pages beat twenty thin ones. Every time.</li>
</ul>
<h2>The order that matters</h2>
<p>Indexing → canonicals → unique titles → speed → structure → links. Do them in that order. A beautifully written article on a page Google can&#39;t index is a tree falling in an empty forest.</p>
<h2>Frequently asked questions</h2>
<p><strong>How do I check whether Google has indexed my site?</strong> Search <code>site:yourdomain.com</code> for a quick count, then use Google Search Console&#39;s Pages report for the real picture — it tells you which URLs are indexed and why the rest aren&#39;t.</p>
<p><strong>Does page speed actually affect rankings?</strong> Yes. Core Web Vitals (LCP, INP, CLS) are a confirmed ranking signal — but the bigger payoff is conversion. Fix the parts users feel: render above-the-fold content server-side so it&#39;s visible before JavaScript loads.</p>
<p><strong>How long until technical SEO fixes show results?</strong> Indexing fixes can surface in days to a few weeks once Google recrawls. Ranking improvements from structure and speed usually take several weeks to months. It compounds — early fixes keep paying off.</p>

## How to Show Up in AI Search

- **Language:** English (en)
- **Slug:** `how-to-show-up-in-ai-search`
- **Excerpt:** Practical GEO: what actually gets you cited in AI Overviews, ChatGPT, and Perplexity — not just ranked in classic Google.
- **Meta description:** A practical guide to GEO: how to get cited by AI Overviews, ChatGPT, and Perplexity — answer-first content, structure, and crawler access.
- **Tags:** SEO, GEO, AI Search

**Body HTML** (paste into source mode):

```html
<p>Search is splitting in two. There&#39;s the blue-links Google you know, and there&#39;s the answer-engine layer — AI Overviews, ChatGPT, Perplexity, Copilot — that increasingly reads your page and <em>summarizes</em> it instead of linking to it. Getting cited there is a different game with different rules. It&#39;s called GEO: generative engine optimization.</p>
<p>The good news: most of GEO is just good SEO done with a little more discipline. Here&#39;s what actually moves the needle.</p>
<h2>Let the AI crawlers in</h2>
<p>You can&#39;t be cited by a model that can&#39;t read you. Check your <code>robots.txt</code> — if you&#39;re blocking <code>GPTBot</code>, <code>PerplexityBot</code>, <code>Google-Extended</code>, or similar, you&#39;ve opted out of the answer layer. Decide deliberately. For most businesses that want visibility, you allow them.</p>
<p>Then make sure your content is in the server-rendered HTML. Many AI fetchers don&#39;t run JavaScript. If your key text only appears after hydration, the model sees a blank page.</p>
<h2>Answer the question in the first two sentences</h2>
<p>Classic SEO rewards a slow build-up. GEO punishes it. Answer engines lift <em>passages</em> — a self-contained chunk that answers a question. So lead with the answer, then elaborate.</p>
<p>Instead of &quot;In this article we&#39;ll explore the many factors that…&quot;, write &quot;The fastest way to X is Y, because Z.&quot; That sentence is quotable. A model can lift it cleanly and attribute it to you.</p>
<h2>Structure for extraction</h2>
<ul>
<li>Clear headings phrased as the questions people actually ask.</li>
<li>Short paragraphs and lists — easy to lift, easy to cite.</li>
<li>One idea per section, with the conclusion at the top of it.</li>
<li>Concrete numbers, dates, and named methods. Specificity reads as authority.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Build entity clarity with structured data</h2>
<p>AI search engines resolve <em>entities</em> — who you are, what you do, how things relate. <code>Person</code>, <code>Organization</code>, <code>Article</code>, and <code>BreadcrumbList</code> schema (in your server-rendered HTML, not injected late by JS) help a model connect your name to your expertise with confidence. This is the difference between being mentioned and being <em>cited</em>.</p>
<h2>Authority still wins</h2>
<p>None of this replaces being genuinely good. Models, like Google, lean on signals of trust: real expertise, a consistent identity across the web, being referenced by others. GEO gets you readable and quotable; authority gets you chosen.</p>
<h2>The GEO checklist</h2>
<ul>
<li><input disabled="" type="checkbox"> AI crawlers allowed in <code>robots.txt</code></li>
<li><input disabled="" type="checkbox"> Key content server-rendered (no JS-only text)</li>
<li><input disabled="" type="checkbox"> Each page answers its core question in the first 2 sentences</li>
<li><input disabled="" type="checkbox"> Headings match real questions; short, liftable passages</li>
<li><input disabled="" type="checkbox"> <code>Person</code>/<code>Article</code> structured data present</li>
<li><input disabled="" type="checkbox"> A clear, consistent identity across your site and profiles</li>
</ul>
<h2>Frequently asked questions</h2>
<p><strong>What is GEO?</strong> Generative engine optimization — the practice of getting your content read, quoted, and cited by AI answer engines like Google&#39;s AI Overviews, ChatGPT, and Perplexity, rather than just ranked in the classic blue-links results.</p>
<p><strong>Should I block AI crawlers like GPTBot?</strong> Only if you don&#39;t want to be cited. For most businesses seeking visibility, you allow <code>GPTBot</code>, <code>PerplexityBot</code>, and <code>Google-Extended</code> in <code>robots.txt</code>. Blocking them opts you out of the answer layer entirely.</p>
<p><strong>Is GEO different from regular SEO?</strong> It overlaps heavily — the same technical foundation (crawlability, server-rendered content, structured data) powers both. GEO adds discipline: answer-first passages, question-shaped headings, and clear entity signals so a model can lift and attribute your text cleanly.</p>

If you&#39;re weighing classic SEO against AI search, the underlying technical work overlaps heavily — see <a href="/en/blog/technical-seo-for-founders">Technical SEO for Founders</a>, or <a href="/en/contact">let&#39;s talk</a> about your site.</p>

## What an MBA Changed About How I Ship Software

- **Language:** English (en)
- **Slug:** `what-an-mba-changed-about-shipping-software`
- **Excerpt:** Engineering taught me to build it right. Business school taught me to build the right thing — and to know the difference.
- **Meta description:** An engineer's take on what an MBA actually changed about shipping software: opportunity cost, unit economics, and deciding what NOT to build.
- **Tags:** Business, Product, Engineering

**Body HTML** (paste into source mode):

```html
<p>For most of my career, &quot;better software&quot; meant cleaner code, faster pages, fewer bugs. Those still matter. But an MBA reframed a question I&#39;d been answering badly for years: not <em>how</em> to build, but <em>whether</em> to build at all — and what it costs to build this instead of that.</p>
<p>Here&#39;s what actually changed in how I work.</p>
<h2>I started pricing the thing I wasn&#39;t building</h2>
<p>Every feature you ship is a feature you chose over something else. Engineers feel this as a backlog; business school names it: opportunity cost. The most expensive line of code is the one that does a real job adequately while a more valuable job goes undone.</p>
<p>Now, before I build, I ask what <em>else</em> that week could buy. Often the honest answer kills the feature. That&#39;s a win, not a loss — unbuilt software has no maintenance cost, no bugs, and no confusion.</p>
<h2>&quot;It works&quot; is not &quot;it pays&quot;</h2>
<p>I used to evaluate a feature by whether it functioned. Now I look at unit economics: what does this cost to run per user, and what does it return? A feature that&#39;s technically beautiful and quietly loses money on every request is a liability wearing a nice suit.</p>
<p>This changes architecture decisions too. The &quot;scalable&quot; choice isn&#39;t the one that handles a million users you don&#39;t have — it&#39;s the one whose cost curve matches your actual revenue curve.</p>
<h2>Deciding what <em>not</em> to do is the job</h2>
<p>The strongest signal of a senior operator — engineer or founder — is the quality of what they decline. Saying yes is easy and feels productive. Saying no, with a reason, is the actual work of strategy.</p>
<p>I now treat the roadmap as a list of bets, not a list of promises. Each item has a cost, an expected return, and a confidence level. Most don&#39;t survive that framing, and the few that do are worth far more attention.</p>
<h2>Talking to the business, not at it</h2>
<p>The most underrated MBA skill is translation: turning &quot;we need to refactor the auth layer&quot; into &quot;this reduces the risk of an outage that would cost us X and stall the launch.&quot; Same work, but now the person holding the budget can actually weigh it. Engineering that can&#39;t make its case in business terms gets deprioritized — not because it&#39;s wrong, but because it&#39;s illegible.</p>
<h2>The synthesis</h2>
<p>Good engineering and good business sense aren&#39;t opposites; they&#39;re the two halves of shipping something that lasts. Code quality keeps you alive at scale. Business judgment makes sure scale is worth reaching. The best work I do now sits exactly where those two meet.</p>
<h2>Frequently asked questions</h2>
<p><strong>Do engineers need an MBA?</strong> No. But the literacy it gives you — opportunity cost, unit economics, deciding what <em>not</em> to build — is genuinely useful, and you can learn most of it without the degree. The degree just forced the reps.</p>
<p><strong>What was the biggest mindset shift?</strong> Pricing the thing I <em>wasn&#39;t</em> building. Every feature is chosen over an alternative; once you cost that alternative honestly, a lot of &quot;obvious&quot; features quietly fall off the roadmap.</p>
<p><strong>How do I justify technical work to non-technical stakeholders?</strong> Translate it into risk, cost, and revenue. &quot;Refactor the auth layer&quot; becomes &quot;this cuts the risk of an outage that would cost us X and stall the launch.&quot; Same work — now the budget holder can weigh it.</p>
