For most of my career, "better software" meant cleaner code, faster pages, fewer bugs. Those still matter. But an MBA reframed a question I'd been answering badly for years: not *how* to build, but *whether* to build at all — and what it costs to build this instead of that.

Here's what actually changed in how I work.

## I started pricing the thing I wasn't building

Every feature you ship is a feature you chose over something else. Engineers feel this as a backlog; business school names it: opportunity cost. The most expensive line of code is the one that does a real job adequately while a more valuable job goes undone.

Now, before I build, I ask what *else* that week could buy. Often the honest answer kills the feature. That's a win, not a loss — unbuilt software has no maintenance cost, no bugs, and no confusion.

## "It works" is not "it pays"

I used to evaluate a feature by whether it functioned. Now I look at unit economics: what does this cost to run per user, and what does it return? A feature that's technically beautiful and quietly loses money on every request is a liability wearing a nice suit.

This changes architecture decisions too. The "scalable" choice isn't the one that handles a million users you don't have — it's the one whose cost curve matches your actual revenue curve.

## Deciding what *not* to do is the job

The strongest signal of a senior operator — engineer or founder — is the quality of what they decline. Saying yes is easy and feels productive. Saying no, with a reason, is the actual work of strategy.

I now treat the roadmap as a list of bets, not a list of promises. Each item has a cost, an expected return, and a confidence level. Most don't survive that framing, and the few that do are worth far more attention.

## Talking to the business, not at it

The most underrated MBA skill is translation: turning "we need to refactor the auth layer" into "this reduces the risk of an outage that would cost us X and stall the launch." Same work, but now the person holding the budget can actually weigh it. Engineering that can't make its case in business terms gets deprioritized — not because it's wrong, but because it's illegible.

## The synthesis

Good engineering and good business sense aren't opposites; they're the two halves of shipping something that lasts. Code quality keeps you alive at scale. Business judgment makes sure scale is worth reaching. The best work I do now sits exactly where those two meet.
